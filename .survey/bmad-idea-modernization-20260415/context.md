# Context: bmad-idea modernization

## Workflow Context
Structured ideation shows up before formal planning in three recurring lanes:

1. **Product planning / discovery** — teams move from raw ideas into problem framing, customer/job framing, concept statements, and experiment candidates before they write a PRD or roadmap item. Representative sources: Atlassian Team Playbook problem-framing and elevator-pitch plays, Teresa Torres continuous discovery, and SVPG discovery-vs-delivery writing.
2. **GTM / marketing / storytelling** — teams translate rough offers into positioning, audience pain framing, value propositions, and narrative briefs before they create launch assets or campaigns. Representative sources: April Dunford on positioning, Strategyzer value-proposition tools, StoryBrand.
3. **Game concepting** — teams shape game concept notes, design pillars, player fantasy, core loops, and lightweight GDD direction before milestone planning. Representative sources: Nuclino GDD guidance and Jesse Schell's Art of Game Design framing.

Across all three lanes, the durable pattern is the same: the highest-leverage skill is usually a **router that chooses one framing mode and one next artifact**, not a static catalog of personas or commands.

## Affected Users
| Role | Responsibility | Skill Level |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| Founder / product lead | Turn vague opportunity ideas into a problem statement, target user, and next validation artifact | mixed |
| Product manager / strategist | Frame problem, audience, and opportunity before PRD / roadmap work | intermediate-advanced |
| Designer / UX researcher | Run empathy, concept, and workflow framing before solution specs | intermediate-advanced |
| Product marketer / GTM lead | Convert rough offer into positioning, message architecture, and story brief | intermediate-advanced |
| Game designer / creative director / indie team | Turn a concept into pillars, hook, player fantasy, and GDD-ready direction | mixed |
| Consultant / agency operator | Normalize ambiguous client inputs into one reusable brief for delivery teams | advanced |

## Current Workarounds
1. Teams stitch together whiteboards, docs, and templates: brainstorm in Miro/FigJam, summarize in Notion/Docs, then manually turn the output into PRDs, launch briefs, decks, or GDD notes.
2. Users keep ad hoc prompt libraries for brainstorming, positioning, concept brief writing, and storytelling because there is no one clear intake-and-routing skill.
3. Teams often jump directly into planning or roadmap breakdown, then backfill rationale later when the initial framing was thin.
4. Storytelling and buy-in artifacts still frequently end up as docs or slides even when ideation started elsewhere.

## Adjacent Problems
- Weak traceability from idea framing to downstream artifacts and assumptions
- Handoff gaps between ideation and `bmad`, `task-planning`, `marketing-automation`, and `bmad-gds`
- Method-selection overload: users do not know whether they need problem framing, audience framing, concept differentiation, game pillars, or story packaging
- Facilitator dependence: generic workshop methods produce lots of notes but not one clear next artifact

## User Voices
- “Product discovery is about tackling the risks before you write a line of code.” — SVPG, discovery-vs-delivery framing, useful as evidence that this skill should sit before implementation planning: https://www.svpg.com/discovery-vs-delivery/
- Continuous discovery guidance from Teresa Torres emphasizes repeatable discovery habits rather than one-off brainstorm dumps: https://www.producttalk.org/2021/08/continuous-discovery-habits/
- April Dunford's positioning framing highlights the need to connect a product to a defined audience and differentiated value before downstream launch work: https://www.aprildunford.com/obviously-awesome/
- StoryBrand's “If you confuse, you'll lose” is relevant because many early-stage asks are really message-clarity and narrative-framing problems: https://storybrand.com/blog/if-you-confuse-youll-lose/
- Jesse Schell's game-design framing underscores that concept work should start from intended player experience, not just feature enumeration: https://www.artofgamedesign.com/
